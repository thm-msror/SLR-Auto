Towards Holistic Language-video Representation: the language model-enhanced
MSR-Video to Text Dataset

YUCHEN YANG and YINGXUAN DUAN, Swiss Federal Institute of Technology Lausanne, Switzerland

A more robust and holistic language-video representation is the key to pushing video understanding forward. Despite the improvement

in training strategies, the quality of the language-video dataset is less attention to. The current plain and simple text descriptions and

the visual-only focus for the language-video tasks result in a limited capacity in real-world natural language video retrieval tasks

where queries are much more complex. This paper introduces a method to automatically enhance video-language datasets, making

them more modality and context-aware for more sophisticated representation learning needs, hence helping all downstream tasks.

Our multifaceted video captioning method captures entities, actions, speech transcripts, aesthetics, and emotional cues, providing

detailed and correlating information from the text side to the video side for training. We also develop an agent-like strategy using

language models to generate high-quality, factual textual descriptions, reducing human intervention and enabling scalability. The

method’s e￿ectiveness in improving language-video representation is evaluated through text-video retrieval using the MSR-VTT

dataset and several multi-modal retrieval models.

1 INTRODUCTION

Video understanding is becoming fundamental and the next frontier for many real-world applications, including

autonomous driving, video retrieval, and the recent advancement in multi-modal generative models. A more robust and

holistic language-video representation is the key to pushing video understanding forward.

With multi-modal contrastive learning using heterogeneous web data becoming a success in image-text representation

and contrastive learning is now widely used for language-video representation. Such a task normally consists of 4

steps: Video representation extraction; Textual representation extraction; Feature embedding and matching; and feature

representation training.

As large-scale pertaining is proven e￿ective for many complex tasks, a rising amount of research focuses on

transferring the existing text-image knowledge to improve language-video representation. Works such as CLIP4Clip[22]

and CLIP2Video[9], using CLIP[26] for large-scale pretraining, achieve state-of-the-art results on various language-video

datasets. Despite the success of CLIP-based models, these methods still treat the language-video representation as

Another thread of work, such as MMT[10] and HiT[21], picks up speci￿cally the multi-modal information, such

embedding on the video’s side, these works open up the door for more diverse and detailed information for the overall

Moreover, the lack of high-quality language-video datasets is gradually being noticed. Early language-video datasets,

such as MSR-VTT[41], use manual annotation. The labour-intensive and expensive annotation consists of only plain and

basic descriptions of the most obvious visual elements within the video. Some following datasets, like HowTo100M[24],

start to use automatic speech recognition (ASR) to make transcripts and construct the video-text pairs for training. The

automated method has greatly enlarged the size of the dataset. However, transcripts as video descriptions only work for

very limited types of videos - like instruction videos, which makes the dataset not representative of real-world semantic

diversity and complexity. To ￿x the problem of poor semantic correlation between the text and video, recent works

[36, 42] have started to deploy automated video captioning and ASR together to make more detailed and comprehensive

text descriptions. The result of such works proved that providing more correlated details on the text side does improve

overall language-video representation and bene￿t downstream tasks.

However, videos are much more complex than just visual and speech. As the expectation on the language-video

representation gets higher, more multi-modal information is extracted from the video representation side. On the other

hand, the basic visual captioning and speech information on the text side failed to provide matching information on a
more subtle or detailed level. This could then become the source of the next round of poor semantic correlation. Can
there be a solution to construct text descriptions that highly correlate to the vast variety of information from
the video side in a ￿exible and automated manner? Answering this question could be the key to achieving a more
robust and holistic language-video representation. To tackle the issue, this work focuses on three main components:

Firstly, we introduce a new method that can be used to improve any existing video-language dataset to be more

modality and context-aware. At the core of this method is a multifacet video captioning method to capture not only the

basics such as the entities, actions, and speech transcripts but also the important and subtle aesthetics and emotional

cues from video. The design of this automated captioning method ensures a ￿exible coverage of di￿erent aspects of the

video and aims to always provide a matching amount of information compared to the video representation side.

Secondly, we compared di￿erent strategies for using the language model to create high-quality and factual textual

descriptions and constructed an agent-like strategy to utilise the language model to ensure the text descriptions are

of requirement and hallucination-free. Incorporating the language model also minimises human intervention in the

process and ensures this can be applied to the dataset at scale.

Thirdly, we assess the feasibility and e￿ect of such a method on overall language-video embedding through an

important downstream task text-video retrieval. In the speci￿c scope of this work, we use the early but open and

representative MSR-VTT as the base dataset, and several representative multi-modal text-video retrieval models without

pretraining to better understand the change brought by the dataset improvement.

2 RELATED WORK

2.1 Language-video Datasets

Language-video data pairs are always the key foundation to enable cross-modal representation and understanding.

There are roughly three phases of language-video datasets development. In the initial phase, datasets are manually

annotated and small. One example is MSR-VTT[41], an open domain dataset with 10k video and text pairs from human

annotators for what roughly happens in the video. Another frequently used one is focused a bit more on the storytelling

perspective, LSMDC[30] contains about 202 movies with either movie script or from transcribed DVS (descriptive

video services). More domain-speci￿c datasets like YouCook2[46] contain 2000 long untrimmed videos from 89 cooking

recipes; on average, each distinct recipe has 22 videos.

Progressing with the maturing ASR technologies, as well as the popularised pretraining method, language-video

datasets in the second phase are getting much larger, and have started to deploy automatic methods for annotation to

cover the large-scale pretraining needs. HowTo100M[24] constructs such pairs using the video and their narration.

Using ASR greatly reduces the cost, and it features a total of 136M video clips with captions sourced from 1.2M YouTube

videos. This opens the door to large-scale language-video datasets and enables pretraining beyond text-image (such

as CLIP). However, these large-scale datasets su￿er from issues brought about by the low correlations in semantics

between the language and the video. In many other cases aside from instructional or tutorial videos, ASR-captured

speech or narration texts may not re￿ect what is happening visually on the screen.

In the most recent phase, works start to explore ways to incorporate more automatic tools and improve the semantic

alignment of the text descriptions and the videos. Recent works [36, 43] have started to incorporate ASR, image

captioning tools, and language models to automatically create more correlated text descriptions for the video. However,

these new attempts only focus on the coarser level description of key events happening in the video visually and the

speech aspect. More intricate details and subtle nuanced elements such as aesthetics and emotional cues are not touched

at all.

2.2 Video Captioning

Yuchen et al.

Video-to-text (V2T) technology has evolved signi￿cantly in recent years, driven by advancements in deep learning and

arti￿cial intelligence[6]. V2T models have achieved impressive results on a variety of benchmarks. They can be used for

a wide range of applications, such as video captioning and video retrieval.

The ￿eld of video captioning has experienced growing interest from both computer vision and natural language

processing researchers[28], as it aims to generate text descriptions of video content. Some previous works concatenate

di￿erent visual features to obtain the video description [34, 45].

Another approach consists of using multi-modal pretraining models. The work of Ging et al. [12] involves di￿erent

levels of granularity and modalities to build the Cooperative Hierarchical Transformer (COOT), which includes

an attention-aware feature aggregation layer that utilizes the local temporal context. This method improves the

performance of the video captioning model but does not consider some important modalities such as background

sounds and conversations. VideoBert[32] and ActBert[47] employ on o￿-the-shelf automatic speech recognition (ASR)

models to gather large repositories of paired video sequences and textual annotations. Both models utilize a BERT-style

objective function to simultaneously train the video and text encoders.

To obtain more detailed descriptions, some works seek help from language models, Seo et al. proposed an end-to-end

generative pretraining model [31] which is able to generate sentences based on the multi-modal context. They utilize

the future utterance as an additional text source to address the absence of captions in unlabeled videos. Other works,

such as [4, 5], focused on directly applying language model-based work￿ow to annotate videos in more comprehensive

detail. While adding more details to the descriptions, these end-to-end methods tend to focus still solely on visual

modality and sometimes encounter the inevitable hallucination problem.

2.3 Multi-modal Text-video Representation

Oftentimes, video-and-language representation utilizes contrastive learning to align video and text features within a

joint latent space based on their semantic similarities. Aside from the spatio-temporal features, videos also encompass

attributes in various modalities such as audio, motion, and Optical Character Recognition (OCR), and motion information.

Some works, such as MMT[10] and MDMMT[15], have studied the importance of including multi-modal video

features. Di￿erent "Experts" are incorporated for these modalities, with each "Expert" being a specialized encoder

to extract features from a certain modality. In these works, the extracted multi-model features on the video side are

combined with the spatial-temporal features to enhance the semantic representations. More speci￿cally, the di￿erent

modality features can include scene embeddings, face features, motion features, audio features, and Optical Character

Recognition (OCR).

However, with the more detailed features from the video side, video-and-language representation is facing a mismatch

in fundamental aspects: the text features that are used to align with the video features are often lacking in semantic

richness. Most of the methods and datasets are still pairing videos with plain and basic text descriptions.

Therefore, we would like to bridge this gap by introducing a robust way to build a more semantically aligned dataset

with more holistic narrative elements and details. As explained in the work of Pérez-Mayos [25], by optimizing the

annotation of the training material, the quality of video-and-language representation can be improved.

3 METHOD

3.1 The MSR-VTT Dataset

The base dataset we used for this work is MSR-VTT (Microsoft Research Video to Text). It is a classic language-video

benchmark proposed by Xu et al. [41] in 2016. It was compiled by gathering over two hundred popular search queries

from YouTube, with over one hundred videos associated with each query. MSR-VTT o￿ers in total 10,000 video clips

each lasting 10 to 20 seconds. Every clip is annotated with approximately 20 natural sentences by Amazon Mechanical

Turk. It sums up to 200,000 sentences for all the videos, making it one of the most classic datasets in terms of both

sentence and vocabulary count. Covering 20 diverse categories, the videos span a wide spectrum of topics to represent
the real-world situation fairly (Fig ??). However, as can be seen in Fig ??, the annotation vastly focuses on the plain visual
happenings in the video. We consider this obvious lack of holistic details in the description to be a great opportunity to

test and evaluate our method.

3.2 Multifacet Video Captioning

Whatever type of videos we work with, they are essentially an audiovisual story. The most important narrative elements

in a story consist of plot, dialogue, setting, characters, point of view, (emotional) tone, and style [2]. They are crucial for

the understanding and navigation of video content. Despite the signi￿cant progress in video captioning techniques,

existing language-video dataset often fails to capture most of these elements. Recent works, such as InternVid [36],

using language models to generate more in-depth and diverse annotations still fail to come about this task from the

narrative perspective - either coming up with arbitrary new details or not controlling the model’s hallucinations. To

address these limitations and maintain the bene￿ts of multi-modality, we propose a new video captioning pipeline that
focuses on and is guided by the key narrative aspects, with the help of language models (see Table ?? for a comparison).

3.2.1 Visual Captioning. The task of getting the main visual annotation covering the plot, setting, and characters is

converted into a sequential image captioning task. There are many choices for image captioning, such as state-of-the-art

close-set segmenters like Segformer[40] and Oneformer[39], which e￿ciently provide detailed semantic descriptions.

CLIPSeg[23], an open-set segmenter, goes beyond conventional categories, generating more creative captions. Also SSA

(Semantic Segment Everything) [3] distinguishes itself by segmenting and classifying every pixel, yielding exceptionally

detailed descriptions.

Our ￿rst attempt was to use SSA since it inherits from SAM (SegmentAnything Model) [13] the powerful ability of

arbitrary object segmentation, and also provides dense open-vocabulary category annotations. However, SSA seems to

be unsuitable for captioning tasks with images that have intricate backgrounds or excessive detail, leading to captions

that are dominated by background elements and fail to adequately capture the main objects in the scene 5.

Fig. 5. Example of segmentation using SSA

Then we decided to utilize the BLIP model to describe our image sequence frames. BLIP [18] introduces a pretraining

Vision-Language Processing (VLP) framework that harnesses the power of noisy web data to build robust, generalizable,

and data-e￿cient models. By leveraging a dual-stream architecture and a three-stage pre-training process, BLIP

e￿ectively fuses visual and linguistic information, achieving superior performance on a variety of VL tasks compared to

traditional methods. BLIP’s ability to learn from diverse data, including noisy web sources, demonstrates its robustness

to real-world variations, while its data e￿ciency streamlines the training process and reduces computational costs.

These advantages position BLIP as a promising framework for advancing VL research and enabling machines to bridge

the gap between vision and language with unprecedented accuracy and robustness.

We extract four frames per second from the video and employ the BLIP model to identify objects and provide a

3.2.2 Dialogue Captioning. Whisper[27]: propose a novel approach to speech recognition that utilizes large quantities

of unlabeled speech data and weak supervision signals. This approach stands in contrast to traditional supervised

learning methods that require carefully curated datasets of labelled audio and text pairs. By leveraging weak supervision,

the proposed model is able to learn from a much broader range of data, including noisy and uncurated sources, leading

to improved robustness and generalization capabilities.

The core of the proposed model is a transformer-based architecture that is trained to predict the most likely transcript

for a given audio clip. The training process is guided by weak supervision signals, which consist of coarse alignment

information between audio and text segments. These alignment signals provide the model with a general sense of the

temporal correspondence between speech and text, allowing it to learn linguistic patterns and acoustic features without

the need for explicit transcription labels.

The proposed approach o￿ers several advantages over traditional supervised learning methods. First, it enables

the training of robust speech recognition models from a vast amount of unlabeled data, which is signi￿cantly more

accessible than labelled data. Second, weak supervision relaxes the need for manual data curation and annotation,

making the training process more e￿cient and scalable. Third, the model’s ability to learn from diverse and noisy data

leads to improved generalization performance, making it more robust to real-world audio conditions.

To transcribe multilingual audio into English, we ￿rst extract the audio from the video using AudioSegment1. Then,

the original language is not English, we set the task parameter to "translate" which enables the transcribe function to

3.2.3 Tone Captionning. In the context of audiovisual understanding, the tone usually indicates the overall emotional
cue within the content ??. In this work, we implement EmoNet?? for tone detection in videos. It is a deep learning-based
framework for estimating continuous valence and arousal levels from faces in naturalistic conditions. this approach

utilizes a 3D CNN to extract features from facial images and an RNN to model the temporal dynamics of facial expressions.

We are able to recognize 8 key emotions, including neutral, disgust, happy, anger, contempt, sad, fear and surprise. Our

approach for this model consists of extracting 2 frames per second and annotating the emotion of each frame, then

counting the total number of times for each emotion to appear. Finally, the most frequently appeared emotion as used

as the base tone for the video.

3.2.4

capture the stylistic features but to showcase the improvement of a more holistic language-video dataset would bring

details. For the ease of the experiment, we choose the dominant colour as the stylistic feature to work with, as it is one

3.3 Language Model Enhancement

Using language models to directly generate annotations for audiovisual content can be extremely costly and sometimes

hard to align with factual information. Instead, the language model in this work is used as an agent. With the visual,

dialogue, tone, and style annotations obtained, we resort to using language to automatically combine them to generate

a comprehensive and accurate video description.

Llama 2 [7] is a trained large language model boasting impressive capabilities in conversation and text generation.

Developed by Meta AI, it aims to bridge the gap between large, closed-source models and more readily available yet

less powerful ones. Unlike many language models, Llama 2’s code and pre-trained weights are entirely open-source,

allowing researchers and developers to freely access and modify it. This fosters transparency and collaboration within

the AI community. Furthermore, Llama 2’s multilingual capabilities facilitate cross-cultural understanding, emotional

intelligence fosters empathy, and domain adaptability caters to speci￿c tasks. Built on the foundation of its predecessor,

Llama 1, Llama 2 surpasses it in many aspects. It demonstrates strong performance in conversational tasks like question
answering, text generation, and summarization. In our study, we employ the ￿ne-tuned 7B chat model of Llama 2 3 to
combine the multifaceted annotations extracted by expert models in the pipeline and generate the improved caption for

MSR-VTT.

the quality, reliability, relevance, and even bias of the generated text. Therefore, it is essential to carefully consider

di￿erent prompt strategies when employing language models for video captioning tasks. Various prompt strategies can

4 EXPERIMENT

We select the popular downstream task, text-to-video retrieval, to test the actual improvement of a more detailed and

holistic annotation in the training dataset for language-video representation learning. The experiments are conducted

based on the original and improved MSR-VTT dataset. More details for the experiment setup are listed in the following

sections:

4.1 Model Selection

This work assumes that with more information becoming available through multi-modal expert encoders on the video

side, the whole language-video representation learning process is hindered by the lack of correlating information on

the text side. The assumption is that regardless of the detailed strategies used for the training process, the more detailed

and matching information from the text side would improve the overall training.

For this, we have ignored models that do not stress the multi-modal features from the video side and only focused

on the multi-modal models. Most multi-modal models focus on extracting multi-modal representations with di￿erent

extractors (also known as experts) and then fuse them to obtain the ￿nal video features for matching with the text side

features. On the other hand, we want to evaluate and compare the results brought by only the improvement presented

in the new language-video pairs, so we purposefully ignored the models that depend on large-scale pretraining to

emphasise that. On top of these, based on the code availability, we chose MMT[10], HCQ[33], and T2VLAD[35] for our

experiment. The three models all have multiple experts on the video representation side and have di￿erent alignment

and training strategies, which make them perfect for testing our hypnosis.

5 CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK

In conclusion, this paper presents advancements in the realm of video-language datasets and their corresponding

applications. Firstly, we introduced a novel method to enhance existing video-language datasets, making them more

modality and context-aware. This multifaceted video captioning method captures a wide range of elements, including

entities, actions, speech transcripts, aesthetics, and emotional cues, ensuring a comprehensive and balanced repre-

sentation of the video content. This modular design also ensures the ￿exibility to make semantic details in the text

descriptions correlate with the video representation and alignment used in various multi-modal models for training the

language-video representation.

Secondly, we explored various strategies for employing language models to generate high-quality, factual textual

descriptions. By developing an agent-like strategy that leverages the language model, we ensured that the descriptions

meet the required standards and are free from hallucinations. This approach also reduces human intervention, enabling

the method to be applied at scale.

Thirdly, we evaluated the feasibility and impact of our method on overall language-video embedding through the

downstream task of text-video retrieval. Using the MSR-VTT dataset and several representative multi-modal text-

video retrieval models without pretraining, we demonstrated the improvements brought by our enhanced dataset are

signi￿cant and cross-model.

Despite these contributions, our study has certain limitations. The scope of our evaluation was restricted to the

MSR-VTT dataset and a limited number of models. Future work should expand the size of the study to include a

broader range of models and datasets, providing a more comprehensive understanding of the method’s e￿ectiveness.

Additionally, our evaluation relied heavily on quantitative metrics such as recall at k (r@k), which may not fully capture

the nuanced improvements in the dataset. Incorporating human evaluation in future studies will help address this

potential bias and provide a more holistic assessment of the dataset’s quality.

Overall, our work lays a robust foundation for further research and development in video-language datasets, o￿ering

promising directions for enhancing the richness and applicability of these datasets in various multimedia and AI

applications.
